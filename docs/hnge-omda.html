I. Executive Summary

This memo is prepared for Omada Health's internal use as both a financial benchmarking exercise and a strategic planning input. Hinge Health has emerged as the defining public comp for digital health platforms serving employer and health plan populations — the company most investors and analysts reach for when sizing Omada's valuation potential. Understanding precisely where Omada sits relative to that benchmark, on every relevant financial dimension, is a prerequisite for credible internal planning and for shaping the investor narrative going forward.

The benchmarking objective is quantitative and primary-source-grounded: where does Omada's revenue, margin, cash flow, and operating efficiency stand today relative to Hinge Health, and how has each trajectory evolved across all reported periods? The strategic planning objective is forward-looking: which specific financial and operational levers — hardware margin structure, incremental profit conversion, FCF generation — most directly influence Omada's path to multiple expansion, and what does the realistic timeline look like based on current guidance? This memo answers both questions with audited figures throughout.

Valuation note: EV calculations use late-March 2026 market prices — HNGE ~$41–42/sh and OMDA ~$13–14/sh. All multiples reflect the current trading environment.

Summary of Key Findings
Revenue Scale & Acceleration — Both Companies: HNGE generated $587.9M in FY2025 revenue (+51% YoY), 2.3× OMDA's $260.2M (+53%). Both companies achieved their strongest reported growth rates in FY2025. At guided trajectories, the absolute revenue gap widens from ~$328M today to ~$420M by end of FY2026 — the relative scale gap holds constant at approximately 2.3×.
The EV Multiple Gap is Justified — and Not Simply a Revenue Story: At current prices, HNGE trades at ~3.9× forward EV/Revenue vs. OMDA's ~1.8×. The key insight is that on profitability-anchored multiples the gap is far more extreme: HNGE at ~16.0× trailing EV/FCF vs. OMDA at ~46×. Counterintuitively, OMDA is more expensive than HNGE on a cash-flow basis despite the lower revenue multiple — because each dollar of OMDA's revenue converts to far less cash.
Incremental Margin Quality — A Real Gap, But Not 10×: HNGE retained approximately 73 cents of every incremental revenue dollar as Non-GAAP operating income in FY2025. Using the same swing methodology for OMDA — Adj. EBITDA moved from $(29.4M) in FY2024 to +$6.5M in FY2025, a $35.9M swing on $90.4M of revenue growth — yields approximately 40 cents per incremental revenue dollar. The gap is real (~1.8×) but bridgeable. The absolute profit gap — $119.5M vs. $6.5M — places OMDA approximately 3–4 years behind HNGE's current profitability scale.
Gross Margin Structure — A Structural Limitation to Gross Margin Expansion: OMDA's services-only GAAP gross margin of 78.5% is already competitive with HNGE's 80%, demonstrating parity on the core care delivery model. The blended figure of 65.7% reflects a deliberate model choice — connected devices are clinically essential to OMDA's cardiometabolic and GLP-1 programs. The resulting 14.3-point gap versus HNGE is a known architectural feature of the current business model, and is expected to persist as long as device-dependent programs remain a primary growth driver.
Rule of 40 — OMDA Likely Exits Elite Tier in FY2026: HNGE scored 81 on the Rule of 40 in FY2025 (company self-reported). OMDA scored 58, crossing the 40 threshold for the first time. However, at FY2026 guided growth rates (~22%) with limited FCF expansion, OMDA's Rule of 40 is estimated to fall to approximately 27 — re-entering the Growth Investment Phase below the elite threshold.
No Driver Closes Within the FY2026 Guidance Window: A systematic review of seven root drivers — operating margin, FCF margin, gross margin, revenue scale, incremental margin quality, Rule of 40, and GLP-1 concentration risk — shows that zero of these gaps close meaningfully within the next twelve months based on published guidance.
II. Company Overviews
Omada Health, Inc. (NASDAQ: OMDA)

Omada Health is a virtual "Between-Visit Care" platform addressing cardiometabolic conditions — diabetes prevention, diabetes, hypertension, weight management — plus musculoskeletal care. OMDA's model is distinctly human-led: Care Teams of health coaches, clinical specialists, and licensed PTs deliver personalized care, augmented by AI and connected devices. Revenue is tied to member enrollment milestones and outcomes-based billing.

OMDA's business model carries two structural gross margin characteristics that differ from HNGE's. First, human care team labor — health coaches, clinical specialists, and licensed PTs — scales roughly with member volume, creating a cost structure that limits the pace of services margin expansion. Second, connected devices (scales, blood pressure monitors, CGMs) are clinically integral to OMDA's cardiometabolic and GLP-1 programs; in FY2025 the device segment generated $19.2M of revenue against $37.4M of COR, a net cost that pulls the blended GAAP margin below the services-only rate of 78.5%.

Omada is the clear enterprise leader in GLP-1 companion care, having scaled from 50,000 to 150,000 GLP-1-enrolled members during 2025. IPO proceeds: ~$161M net (June 2025). Balance sheet: $222.0M cash, zero debt (MidCap facility fully repaid). FY2025 was the first year of both FCF and Adj. EBITDA profitability for OMDA in its history.

Hinge Health, Inc. (NYSE: HNGE)

Hinge Health is a digital musculoskeletal (MSK) care platform delivering AI-powered exercise therapy, an FDA-cleared wearable device (Enso), and virtual care teams to self-insured employers and health plans. The company bills on a per-member-per-year subscription basis embedded within clients' medical spend, providing billing stickiness that is meaningfully different from discretionary benefit budgets.

Critically, HNGE's platform is purely software-delivered — no ongoing hardware dependency at scale — enabling a GAAP gross margin of 80% in FY2025 (83% on a non-GAAP basis). The company's AI-powered TrueMotion technology has reduced human physical therapist hours per member by approximately 95% versus traditional in-person PT. As of December 31, 2025, Hinge served 2,830 clients, had approximately 25 million contracted lives, 782,890 active members, a 97% client retention rate, and NDR of 116%.

IPO proceeds: ~$256M net (June 2025). Balance sheet: $478.8M cash and marketable securities, zero debt. The company authorized a $250M share repurchase program in November 2025 and executed $65M of buybacks in Q4 2025 — made possible by $179.6M of FY2025 FCF.

Incremental Margin Analysis

The incremental operating margin — the percentage of each additional revenue dollar that flows through to operating profit — is one of the most powerful expressions of a platform's operating leverage. It is less susceptible to base effects than percentage margin changes and more directly reflects the unit economics of scaling.

For HNGE in FY2025: Non-GAAP operating income moved from $(26.1M) to +$119.5M — a swing of $145.6M. Revenue moved from $390.4M to $587.9M — an increase of $197.5M. Incremental Non-GAAP operating margin: $145.6M ÷ $197.5M = approximately 73 cents per incremental revenue dollar.

For OMDA in FY2025: Adj. EBITDA moved from $(29.4M) in FY2024 to +$6.5M in FY2025 — a full swing of $35.9M on $90.4M of revenue growth — yielding approximately 40 cents per incremental revenue dollar. OMDA is converting 40¢ of every new revenue dollar into profit — a credible starting point, but well below HNGE's 73¢. The more consequential difference is absolute scale: HNGE generated $119.5M of non-GAAP operating income vs. OMDA's $6.5M of Adj. EBITDA — placing OMDA approximately 3–4 years behind at current growth rates.

Funded entirely from operating FCF and existing cash — no balance sheet impairment. Management has also begun reporting FCF per share ($2.12 for FY2025) and stated intent to grow it "through both increased cash flow and buybacks" — a deliberate shift in investor communication toward per-share value creation. 2026 diluted share guidance of 85–87M shares is stated "excluding further repurchases," signaling additional execution is expected.

The repurchase program influences HNGE's multiple favorability through two distinct channels. First, mechanically: retiring shares reduces market cap and therefore EV at any given price, directly compressing EV/Revenue and EV/FCF multiples over time without requiring stock price movement. With $185M of authorization remaining and FCF continuing to build, this share count reduction effect compounds — HNGE's forward multiples improve not only because revenue and FCF are growing, but because the denominator is being actively managed downward. Second, structurally: capital return at scale signals to institutional investors that FCF generation exceeds reinvestment needs. That framing attracts longer-duration holders who anchor to earnings quality rather than growth, supporting multiple stability precisely as revenue growth decelerates toward the 25% guided rate. Management's deliberate shift to FCF-per-share reporting reinforces this investor base migration.

What the timing signals: Authorizing a $250M buyback at five months post-IPO — before lockup expirations are fully resolved, before the investor base has fully stabilized — is an unusually confident capital allocation decision. It is only credible when FCF is large, durable, and management has high conviction in intrinsic value. The fact that HNGE deployed $65M in a single quarter while ending the year with $479M in cash and zero debt illustrates a balance sheet that is generating surplus capital beyond its organic growth needs.
The SBC offset dynamic: Management explicitly framed the buyback as a mechanism to "offset future dilution." HNGE's SBC dilution trended below 3% of shares outstanding in FY2025 and is guided to decline. The buyback converts what would otherwise be a dilutive equity comp program into a net share count reduction — directly accretive to per-share FCF. This is a flywheel available only to businesses generating FCF well in excess of their equity comp obligations.
The OMDA contrast — and what changes it: OMDA's $12.4M of FY2025 FCF makes any equivalent capital return program structurally impossible at this stage. This is not a criticism — it reflects where OMDA sits in its maturation curve. The relevant planning question is what FCF scale unlocks this optionality. At 10% FCF margin on $317M of guided 2026 revenue, OMDA would generate ~$32M of annual FCF — enough to cover organic needs but not capital return. At 15%+ FCF margin on $400M+ revenue (a plausible 2027–2028 scenario), FCF of $60M+ begins to create the conditions for a similar conversation. Until then, the capital return optionality gap is itself a component of the valuation discount.

VII. Gross Margin Structure — Services Parity and the Blended Gap

OMDA's blended GAAP gross margin of 65.7% in FY2025 is frequently cited as a significant gap versus HNGE's 80%. The more useful framing begins one level down: OMDA's services segment — health coaching, clinical specialists, and care delivery — generated a 78.5% GAAP gross margin in FY2025, within 1.5 points of HNGE's 80% on a like-for-like basis. The blended figure reflects the cost structure of connected devices, which are clinically integral to OMDA's cardiometabolic and GLP-1 programs rather than a discretionary component of the model.

What Would Actually Close the Gap

The following conditions would need to be satisfied, likely over a 2027–2028 timeframe, to justify meaningful multiple convergence:

Sustained full-year FCF margins of 10–15%+: Not a single-quarter event. The market requires multiple consecutive years of FCF generation at scale to re-anchor intrinsic value away from revenue-based frameworks.
Gross margin expansion through services efficiency: Services margin has the most meaningful room to run, driven by AI-assisted care delivery, care team leverage, and program mix. Device cost structure is an ongoing feature of the clinical model; efficiency gains there are incremental rather than transformational.
Demonstrated incremental margin improvement toward 60¢+ per revenue dollar: Currently at ~40¢ per incremental revenue dollar, OMDA needs to expand this ratio toward 60¢+ to demonstrate the operating leverage profile that supports earnings multiple re-rating.
GLP-1 revenue diversification: OMDA's GLP-1 concentration needs to be balanced by growth in other chronic condition programs (cholesterol, MSK, behavioral health) that do not carry hardware cost dependencies.
Maintained 20%+ revenue growth alongside improving margins: Multiple expansion requires both levers simultaneously. If revenue growth decelerates to the mid-teens while FCF margins are still in low single digits, the re-rating case weakens.
IX. Competitive Positioning & Strategic Context
Market Intersection

HNGE and OMDA are frequently compared as "digital health peers," but they are structurally different platforms addressing different conditions with different business models. HNGE is a pure-play MSK platform with hardware-enhanced, AI-first delivery. OMDA is a multi-condition cardiometabolic platform with human-led care teams augmented by AI and connected devices. The two are beginning to overlap — OMDA offers Omada for MSK, and HNGE has announced plans to expand beyond MSK — and competitive overlap will intensify over 2026–2028 as both platforms seek to expand their share of employer health benefit budgets.

GLP-1 Dynamic

GLP-1 medications represent the most significant strategic variable for OMDA. Having supported 150,000+ members on GLP-1s, Omada has repositioned as a GLP-1 enabler — launching GLP-1 Flex Care, a new prescribing service, and a cholesterol management program. This is a genuine strategic asset. The risk is concentration: if GLP-1 reimbursement policies change or competitors enter with lower-cost companion programs, OMDA's primary growth engine could be disrupted in ways that HNGE — whose MSK platform has no equivalent drug class dependency — does not face.

For HNGE, GLP-1s are a more nuanced dynamic: weight loss reduces obesity-driven MSK deterioration over the long term, potentially reducing the addressable population. However, GLP-1 use also increases exercise engagement, and Hinge's platform is well-positioned to support members in physical conditioning during and after GLP-1 therapy. Net effect on HNGE's addressable market is not yet quantifiable.

Key Risks by Company
HNGE: Customer concentration (Health Care Services Corp 17%, Elevance 14%, Aetna 12% of FY2024 revenue). Contract terminability by health plan partners. Potential commoditization of virtual MSK as competitors scale. Long-term GLP-1 impact on MSK surgery utilization and addressable market.
OMDA: Connected device cost structure that may scale with GLP-1 program growth. Human care team labor as a factor in services margin expansion pace. Channel partner concentration (Cigna affiliate ~31% of Q1 2025 revenue per S-1). GLP-1 reimbursement policy risk. Single-quarter GAAP profitability history — track record of sustained profitability not yet established.
X. Conclusions
1. HNGE's valuation premium is rationally priced, not excessive, even at corrected lower prices.

At ~$2.88B EV against $587.9M FY2025 revenue, $179.6M FCF, and $119.5M Non-GAAP operating income, HNGE's multiples are high but defensible. EV/FCF of ~16.0× on a business growing 51% with 31% FCF margins is not stretched by institutional quality-growth frameworks. The $250M buyback program — initiated five months post-IPO and funded entirely from operating FCF — further illustrates the degree to which HNGE's capital generation exceeds its organic reinvestment needs.

2. OMDA is counterintuitively more expensive than HNGE on cash flow metrics.

OMDA's ~1.8× forward EV/Revenue looks modest relative to HNGE's ~3.9×. But OMDA's ~46× trailing EV/FCF vs. HNGE's ~16.0× reveals the reality: each dollar of OMDA revenue converts to far less cash. This is the precise reason simple revenue multiple comparisons are insufficient for analyzing these two businesses.

3. Incremental margin is the right metric — but the methodology matters.

HNGE retained ~73¢ of every incremental FY2025 revenue dollar. Using the correct swing methodology for OMDA yields approximately 40¢ per incremental dollar — a real gap (~1.8×), but not the ~10× figure that circulates when the comparison is constructed incorrectly. OMDA's 40¢ incremental margin is a legitimate starting point, and sustaining or expanding it toward 60¢+ is the specific operational path to closing the multiple gap.

4. OMDA's FY2025 profitability milestones are genuine and important — but they mark a starting point.

First FCF-positive year, first Adj. EBITDA-positive year, first GAAP-profitable quarter. These are real inflection signals. But the distance from a first profitable quarter to HNGE's current operating profile — $120M Non-GAAP operating income, $180M FCF, $250M buyback, Rule of 40 of 81 — is measured in years, not quarters.

5. The Rule of 40 forward observation is the most underappreciated risk to OMDA's near-term multiple.

OMDA's Rule of 40 of 58 in FY2025 crossed into elite territory for the first time. But at guided ~22% revenue growth with FCF margins estimated at ~5%, FY2026 Rule of 40 of approximately 27 re-enters Growth Investment Phase — below best-in-class. Institutional Rule-of-40-anchored funds that entered at 58+ may reassess their position as growth decelerates without commensurate FCF expansion. This is a real near-term multiple headwind not yet widely discussed.

6. Both companies are well-positioned for continued long-term value creation.

The goal of this analysis is precision: to identify exactly why the multiples differ, by how much, and whether those gaps are likely to close. The answer is that they are rational, durable within the FY2026 window, and require a specific multi-year execution journey from OMDA to close. That journey is plausible. It is not yet guidable.

Appendix: Definitions, Methodology & Data Sources

Revenue: GAAP total revenue as reported in SEC filings. All figures audited unless noted as preliminary.

Gross Profit / Margin: GAAP: Total Revenue minus Cost of Revenue per income statement. Non-GAAP (both companies): Adds back SBC and D&A allocated to Cost of Revenue.

Adj. EBITDA (OMDA): Directly disclosed by OMDA. Net Loss + interest expense + interest income + fair value of warrants + loss on debt extinguishment + income taxes + SBC + amortization of intangibles + D&A + loss on asset disposal. FY2025 audited: +$6.5M.

Non-GAAP Operating Income (HNGE): GAAP operating income (loss) excluding SBC, employer payroll taxes on SBC, amortization of intangibles, restructuring, and other one-time items. FY2025: $119.5M. HNGE's primary non-GAAP measure. Estimated Adj. EBITDA = Non-GAAP Operating Income + D&A (~$5.1M in FY2025).

Free Cash Flow: Net cash from operating activities minus capex (PP&E purchases) minus capitalized internal-use software costs. Both companies disclose FCF directly in earnings releases.

Incremental Operating Margin: Change in Non-GAAP Operating Income (HNGE) or Adj. EBITDA (OMDA) divided by change in Revenue between FY2024 and FY2025. Represents cents of operating profit per incremental revenue dollar.

Rule of 40: Revenue Growth Rate (YoY %) + FCF Margin (FCF ÷ Revenue). FCF-based throughout for consistency with HNGE's own disclosure (company reported 81 in FY2025 Q4 earnings release).

Enterprise Value: Market Capitalization minus Cash and Cash Equivalents (including marketable securities). HNGE: ~82M diluted shares × ~$41/sh = ~$3.36B mkt cap; less $478.8M net cash = ~$2.88B EV. OMDA: ~58.4M shares × ~$13.50/sh = ~$789M mkt cap; less $222M cash = ~$567M EV. Prices as of late March 2026; refresh before reliance.

Key Observations — Overhang & Trading Dynamics

HNGE — The Buyback as Overhang Defense

This sequencing was almost certainly deliberate: the buyback provided a price floor signal and a visible demand offset as the lockup supply hit. The stock was at ~$41 at expiry and held near that level through December, suggesting the buyback effectively absorbed meaningful selling pressure. The staggered two-tranche structure (1.66M shares August 19, remainder November 18) also limited the initial dilution shock, giving the stock time to re-rate on fundamental performance before the full float opened.
